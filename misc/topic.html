Sensor technology to enable self-driving cars

In order for self-driving cars to make people's lives convenient and reduce accidents that are currently occurring frequently on the road, cars should be able to sense not only the front but also the surroundings at 360° and know what is happening around them. To do so, it is necessary to integrate various types of sensor mechanisms with automobiles. Some of these are already being used in advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS) today, and others are emerging as self-driving cars. It would not be possible to automate a car without using sensors. The six steps of autonomy, from human-driven cars to full self-driving cars, are more specifically desired in the portability section. In this website, we look at sensor technology that enables self-driving cars.

Sensor Mechanism

There are three representative technologies that allow autonomous vehicles to detect their surroundings. It's a lidar, a camera, a radar. Currently, each of these technologies is in a different development stage.

Comparison of sensor technologies used in self-driving cars The simplest of these is radar. Radar is already being used for functions such as adaptive driving control. Radar can also play an important role for self-driving cars. In particular, low-speed situations such as parking and slow-down are mentioned. It can also be used for functions that are executed at high speed, such as lane change on highways. A millimeter wave (mmWave) car radar system, which is an electromagnetic wave with a wavelength of 1 to 10 millimeters and a frequency of 30,000 to 300,000 megahertz (MHz), can calculate the distance, speed, and relative angle of an object using short wave electromagnetic waves. In general, it operates in a frequency band of 77 gigahertz (GHz) and can distinguish up to very small movements. In particular, the advantages of radar can be various. As a proven technology, it can operate reliably regardless of changes in environmental conditions. It is relatively inexpensive because the hardware is compact and achieves economies of scale as an industry-established technology. But there are also fundamental limitations. Among them, the most important thing is the amount of data that can be provided. For this reason, it is necessary to combine a series of sensors rather than relying on only one sensor method for autonomous vehicles. Lidar is a technology that almost all automakers currently include in their development programs, which can complement the car radar system. When a light pulse is emitted from a laser light source, the light is reflected by hitting an object. The distance to the object can be calculated using the time each of these pulses returns to the starting point. This process can be repeated millions of times per second to create a real-time 3D map of the surrounding environment. By doing so, you can identify other cars, road infrastructure, bicycles, pedestrians, and drive away from obstacles. Compared to other sensor methods, the biggest advantage of lidar is that it can generate a bird's eye view, providing a more comprehensive view. Ford is already investing heavily in this technology. The company is Velodyne's HDL-64E line for ongoing self-driving car development and testing It is using the system, and the first model to adopt the system will be released sometime in 2021.

LiDAR

The HDL-64E is a 64-channel system featuring a 360° horizontal viewing angle (FoV) and a distance of up to 120 meters at 26.9° vertical FoV. The number of channels supported is important in relation to the speed of the car. According to Velodine, vehicles with a 32-channel system can drive autonomously up to 57 kph, which can support much higher speeds by doubling the number of channels. The biggest obstacle to the supply of lidar is its high price. (That's why Elon Musk says Lidar is unnecessary) Currently, the LIDA system is priced at thousands of euros. Even if the size of the LIDA system increases, it is still expected to be very expensive. Price is not the only stumbling block. Lyda can accurately create maps around cars, but it does not provide the detail required for other purposes, such as road sign recognition. For such image recognition and classification work, a high-definition camera system should be used as an autonomous vehicle.

Image sensors / Camera

Cameras can be installed front, side, and rear by automobiles and screens can be connected to provide a 360° real-time view of the surroundings. By doing so, it is possible to minimize blind spots, inform that the speed limit is changed, and maintain lanes. The number of cameras required will vary depending on the system's FoV (up to 120° possible) and whether you want to implement a fisheye camera (to provide a panoramic view using an ultra-wide-angle lens). Like any sensor technology, cameras should also consider both advantages and disadvantages. The camera system may distinguish the surroundings in detail, but the depth and distance may be problematic. To understand the exact location of the detected object, you need to know the distance to the object. In addition, cameras can be difficult to distinguish objects in poor visibility (such as bad weather or at night). Another problem that system developers have begun to recognize is that sensor systems can affect other autonomous vehicles. Recently, discussions have been made on whether LIDA does not interfere with the operation of the digital camera system. If so, it can be a serious problem (which could be life-threatening) when self-driving cars approach each other face to face. Many studies are currently being actively conducted on how to combine sensors with self-driving cars in the future. One thing is certain, each of the pros and cons can be compensated by combining various methods (radar, image sensing, lidar, etc.). Various sensors can be combined to implement the various functions required for self-driving cars, while also achieving the redundancy required to make self-driving cars not dangerous.
